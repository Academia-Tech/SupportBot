Рекомендація (не підтверджено)

Newcomers should understand that 3D printing is a hobby that requires a willingness to learn and experiment. It involves tweaking numerous slicer settings (layers, walls, infills, supports) and understanding the physical mechanics of the printer (adjusting temperature, speed, tension). It's a complex learning exercise, not a '3D photocopier,' and users should expect to troubleshoot issues more complex than a simple paper jam. The hobby is best suited for those who enjoy tinkering and problem-solving.

I've always warned people... if they think 3d printing is going to be as easy as 2d printing, or a magic machine to clone 3d objects in a few clicks, a 3d photocopier... this is not the hobby for them. You NEED to be able to play with 101 slicer settings, supports, layers, walls, infills, all sorts of "fun" stuff (who tweaks more than about 3 different 2d printer driver settings? If any?). You NEED to understand all the physical bits that might need to be tightened, loosened, adjusted up/down, in/out, hotter/colder, faster/slower. The inevitable problems are also going to be WAY more complicated than "change ink", "load letter paper in tray 2", or even "open flap 4 and remove paper jam". It's a big learning exercise. If you're one of those "lifelong learning" people, "studying at the university of life", if you're willing to (better still, if you're **hoping to**, **looking forward to**) experiment(ing), **then** this is a hobby you may enjoy. "I just want a printer that always works", really... ain't gonna work. Yet. Maybe one day. If enough of us experiment enough first. 🤷‍♂
